WHO CONTROLS THE BORDER

No single government does. The Global Mobility Authority (GMA) — a private-public coalition formed after the climate collapse agreements of 2041 — replaced all national border systems with a single algorithm: the Mobility Clearance Score (MCS). The GMA is governed by a board of 12 corporate signatories and 4 surviving climate bloc nations. Borders are enforced by AI-operated biometric checkpoints. There are no human guards — only sensor gates and automated detainment pods.

HOW PEOPLE CROSS

A retinal scan at any checkpoint reads your embedded UID microchip, linked to your MovePass profile. The app updates your score in real time. Scores above 850 move freely across all zones. Scores like Rivera's — 612 — allow only local movement within an 80km radius. Scores below 500 trigger "mobility suspension," a form of algorithmic house arrest. There are no lines, no stamps, no agents. The border is invisible and everywhere.

WHAT NO LONGER EXISTS

Passports. Visas. Asylum law. The 1951 Refugee Convention was suspended in 2048 and never reinstated. Citizenship as a legal category was dissolved — replaced by Tier classification based entirely on score. You don't belong to a country anymore. You belong to a bracket. The concept of refuge — the idea that a human being fleeing danger has the right to cross a border — no longer has legal standing anywhere on Earth.

✎ WRITTEN EXPLANATION (330 words)

The MovePass app is an artifact from 2071 — a phone screen belonging to M.C. Rivera, a climate-displaced worker living under Tier 3 Conditional status in what was once Southern California. It represents a border zone transformed not by walls, but by code.

By 2071, the old physical border — the one marked by fences, checkpoints, and agents — no longer exists in its original form. What replaced it is invisible, continuous, and inescapable: the Mobility Clearance Score. The GMA, a corporate-government coalition born from the 2041 Climate Accords, controls all human movement through a single algorithm. Your score determines where you can live, work, and travel. Rivera's 612 keeps her locked in an 80km radius. She cannot reach a Green Zone — a climate-stable region — no matter how dangerous conditions become where she is. The border has no gate. It lives in her phone.

What no longer exists is asylum. The right to seek safety across a border — once protected under international law — was quietly dissolved when the Refugee Convention was suspended during the climate crisis. The crisis gave governments justification to restrict movement at scale, and the GMA institutionalized that restriction permanently. What replaced it is the appeal system: Rivera can submit a digital appeal, which an algorithm will deny in 14 days.

The present made this possible. Credit scoring systems already decide who gets housing. Predictive algorithms already sort people by risk. Biometric data is already collected at airports, on phones, in stores. Climate displacement is already producing millions of people who fall outside existing legal protections. This future is not science fiction — it is an extrapolation of systems already in motion.

The hopeful counter is also real: open-source score auditing movements, Indigenous data sovereignty frameworks, and decentralized ID systems are all being built now — tools designed so that no single authority can own the algorithm that decides where you belong. The question is whether those tools arrive before the walls become invisible.
